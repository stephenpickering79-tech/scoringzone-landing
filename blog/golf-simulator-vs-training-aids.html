Golf Simulator vs Training Aids: Which Is Worth It?

An Honest Cost-Benefit Breakdown

April 9, 2026 · 7 min read · Stephen Pickering

Key takeaway: A home golf simulator costs £10K–£50K+ and is brilliant for full swing practice. But most strokes are lost within 100 yards — where a £50 putting mat and a free practice app deliver better ROI than any simulator.

A mate of mine just spent $25,000 on a home golf simulator. Full enclosure, launch monitor, projector, the lot. He plays virtual Pebble Beach three nights a week. His handicap hasn’t moved in a year.

That’s not a knock on simulators. They’re brilliant for what they do. But if you’re weighing up a golf simulator vs training aids and asking which one will actually lower your scores — the answer probably isn’t what the sim companies want you to hear.

Here’s an honest breakdown of what each option delivers, what it costs, and where the real ROI sits for most club golfers.

What a Golf Simulator Does Well

Full swing data and feedback

This is where simulators genuinely shine. A quality launch monitor — Trackman, GCQuad, Flightscope, or even the more affordable Garmin Approach R10 — gives you ball speed, spin rate, launch angle, carry distance, and club path on every swing. That data is gold for dialling in your distances and understanding your tendencies.

If you’ve never seen your actual carry numbers with each club, a simulator session will change how you think about club selection. MyGolfSpy’s 2025 launch monitor testing found that most amateurs overestimate their carry distances by 10–15 yards. A simulator fixes that in one session.

Play any course in any weather

The fun factor is real. Playing Pebble Beach, St Andrews, or Augusta from your garage on a Tuesday evening is genuinely enjoyable. In climates where winter shuts down golf for months, a sim keeps you swinging. And for golfers who travel a lot or have limited time, it removes the commute to the course.

Club gapping and fitting

Knowing the exact distance gaps between your wedges — pitching wedge, gap wedge, sand wedge, lob wedge — is one of the simplest scoring improvements you can make. A simulator with a decent launch monitor lets you hit 20 balls with each club and see the real numbers. No guessing.

Where Simulators Fall Short

The cost is staggering

A basic home setup — enclosure, mat, net, budget launch monitor, projector, and screen — starts around $5,000 and feels like a compromise. A mid-range setup with a quality launch monitor runs $15,000–$25,000. A premium build with Trackman or GCQuad, a high-end screen, and proper room treatment pushes $40,000–$50,000 or more.

Then there’s the space. You need a room with at least 10 feet of ceiling height, 15 feet of depth, and 12 feet of width. Not many houses have that sitting empty.

According to Golf Digest’s 2025 home simulator buyer’s guide, the average fully installed home sim costs between $15,000 and $30,000 — and that’s before software subscriptions, which run $200–$500 per year for platforms like E6 Connect or GSPro.

It doesn’t replicate real conditions

You’re hitting off a flat mat. Every lie is perfect. There’s no wind. No slope under your feet. No thick rough. No hardpan. No wet bunker sand. The ball flight on screen is a simulation — it’s close, but it’s not the same as watching your ball curve in real air.

For full swing, this matters less. For the short game, it matters enormously. A chip from a tight lie on firm turf plays completely differently to a chip off a simulator mat. The feel, the bounce, the margin for error — none of it transfers.

Short game practice is limited

This is the big one. Most strokes for amateur golfers aren’t lost off the tee or from the fairway. Mark Broadie’s strokes gained research — the gold standard for understanding where scoring happens — shows that amateurs lose a disproportionate number of shots inside 100 yards. A 15-handicapper loses roughly 4–5 shots per round from within 50 yards compared to scratch.

A simulator can help with distance wedges — 60, 80, 100 yards — where carry distance is the key variable. But chipping from 10 yards? Putting with real break and real speed? Bunker shots from actual sand? A sim can’t replicate any of that meaningfully. You’re practising the 40% of the game that costs you fewer strokes while ignoring the 60% that costs you the most.

The Case for Affordable Training Aids

Putting mats ($50–$200)

A decent putting mat — Wellputt, PuttOut, or PUTTR — gives you a consistent surface to groove your stroke at home. The best ones have distance markers and alignment guides. Twenty minutes a day on a putting mat builds the stroke consistency that reduces three-putts on the course.

A 15-handicapper who three-putts 3–4 times per round can realistically save 2–3 strokes just by improving lag putting and short putt consistency. A $100 putting mat pays for itself in a weekend.

Chipping nets ($30–$100)

A backyard chipping net lets you hit real chips with real clubs. You get feedback on strike, trajectory, and landing zone. Unlike a simulator, you’re hitting off real grass (or at least a realistic mat) with real loft and real bounce interaction.

Pair it with a target and a scoring system — how many out of 10 land in the net, how many from 20 yards vs 30 yards — and you’ve got structured practice for the cost of a single sleeve of Pro V1s.

Alignment sticks ($15)

The cheapest and most versatile training aid in golf. Use them for alignment on full shots, as gate drills for putting, as target markers for chipping, or as a visual aid for swing path. Every tour pro has a pair in their bag. They cost less than a round of drinks.

Practice apps (free to $20/month)

This is where training aids have changed most in the last few years. A structured practice app turns a random 30 minutes on the putting green into a scored session with benchmarks, pressure, and tracking. You know exactly what to work on, how you scored, and whether you’re improving week over week.

The cost is negligible — most are free or under $20 per month. Compare that to a $25,000 simulator and the ROI difference is hard to ignore.

Where Your Strokes Are Actually Hiding

The 60% problem

Around 60% of all golf shots happen inside 100 yards. PGA Tour players get up and down about 59% of the time. A 10-handicapper? Around 32%. A 20-handicapper? Just 10%. The gap is massive — and it’s not a gap that a simulator closes.

Tour pros three-putt only about 0.5 times per round. A 15-handicapper averages 3.2 three-putts. From 11–30 feet, amateurs are seven times more likely to three-putt than a tour player. These aren’t full-swing problems. They’re short game problems. And they respond to cheap, targeted practice — not expensive equipment.

Cost per stroke saved

Think about it this way. A $25,000 simulator helps your full swing — which, for most amateurs, is not the primary source of wasted strokes. A $100 putting mat, a $50 chipping net, $15 alignment sticks, and a free practice app target the exact area where your strokes are hiding.

Total cost: under $200. That’s 0.8% of the simulator price, aimed at the part of your game that accounts for the majority of your scoring gap. Even if you already own a simulator, this is money well spent on top of it.

The Smart Combination

Use a sim for what it does best

If you have a simulator — or access to one — use it for full swing work. Dial in your carry distances. Check your club gaps. Work on your driver dispersion. Play virtual rounds for fun and to stay sharp in winter. That’s genuine value.

Scoring Zone has a SIM Lab with challenges built specifically for launch monitors — distance control tests, pitching accuracy ladders, and proximity challenges that give your sim sessions structure and scoring instead of aimless hitting.

Do your short game work on real greens

Then step outside. Practise your chipping on actual turf with actual lies. Putt on real greens with real slope and real speed. Use a putting mat at home between sessions. Score every session. Track every trend.

The golfers who improve fastest aren’t the ones with the most expensive equipment. They’re the ones who practise the right things with structure and measurement. A $50 putting mat used three times a week with scored drills will do more for your handicap than a $50,000 simulator gathering dust in the garage.

Don’t let one tool become the whole plan

The mistake isn’t buying a simulator. The mistake is thinking it covers everything. A sim is a full-swing tool and an entertainment system. It’s not a short game coach. Pair it with real short game practice — or, if you don’t have a sim, skip it entirely and invest in the tools that target where your strokes actually go.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a golf simulator worth the money for improving your game?

It depends on what you’re trying to improve. A golf simulator is excellent for full swing data, club fitting, and playing courses in bad weather. But for most amateurs, the strokes are hiding inside 100 yards — and simulators are poor at replicating real short game conditions. If your goal is lower scores, affordable training aids and a structured practice app will deliver a better return per pound spent than a $15K–$50K simulator.

What are the best affordable golf training aids?

The best value training aids for most amateurs are a quality putting mat ($50–$200), a chipping net ($30–$100), alignment sticks ($15), and a structured practice app (free to $20/month). These target the short game — the part of golf where 60% of your shots happen and where the biggest scoring gains are available. Combined, they cost less than one month’s payment on a home simulator.

Can you practise short game on a golf simulator?

You can practise pitch shots and distance wedges on a simulator with reasonable accuracy, but chipping and putting are severely limited. A simulator mat doesn’t replicate real lies — hardpan, thick rough, tight lies, or bunker sand. Putting on a simulator uses a flat surface with no real green reading. For short game practice to transfer to the course, you need real turf, real slopes, and real feedback.

What’s the smartest way to combine a simulator with training aids?

Use the simulator for what it does best — full swing analysis, club gapping, distance control with irons and woods, and playing courses when you can’t get outside. Then do your short game work on real greens with a putting mat, chipping net, and a structured practice app that scores your sessions. This combination covers the full game without overspending on one tool.
